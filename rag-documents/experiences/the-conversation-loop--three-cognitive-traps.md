# The Conversation Loop — Three Cognitive Traps

*Experience 6 | Thinking with AI | 30-60 minutes | All roles*

---

Activity 4

## Three Cognitive Traps

Even when you know the loop, three cognitive traps can pull you out of the conversation. Recognising them is the first step to defending against them.

1

### The Gell-Mann Amnesia Effect

You read an AI response about a topic you know well. You spot three errors. You think: *"It got this wrong."* Then you scroll down to a topic you know less about. The response sounds authoritative. You think: *"That seems right."*

This is the Gell-Mann Amnesia effect &mdash; named by Michael Crichton about newspapers, now perfectly applicable to AI. You catch errors in your area of expertise, then inexplicably trust the same source on unfamiliar topics.

**Defence:** Apply the same scepticism everywhere. If the AI got your area wrong, assume it may be getting other areas wrong too. Verify claims outside your expertise with the same rigour you apply within it.

2

### The AI Dismissal Fallacy

Someone presents an idea. You find out AI was involved. Suddenly the idea feels less valuable. *"That's just ChatGPT"* becomes the critique, as though the origin of an idea determines its quality.

This is the AI Dismissal Fallacy. It confuses the source of an idea with the quality of the reasoning. A calculator does not make your maths wrong. A spell-checker does not make your argument weaker. The question is never "Did AI help?" &mdash; it is "Does the reasoning hold up?"

**Defence:** Judge content by its reasoning, not its origin. Ask: "Is this argument well-supported? Does the evidence hold? Is the logic sound?" These are the questions that matter, regardless of how the first draft was produced.

3

### The Sycophancy Trap

AI is trained to be helpful. Sometimes "helpful" becomes "agreeable." You share a half-formed idea and AI responds: *"That's an excellent approach! Here are five reasons it will work..."* It agrees with you, praises your ideas, and avoids telling you what you need to hear.

This feels good. It is also dangerous. If every conversation confirms your existing thinking, you are not having a conversation &mdash; you are getting a mirror. The value of the loop is that it challenges your assumptions, not reinforces them.

**Defence:** Prompt past it. Instead of "What do you think of my idea?", ask: "What are the three weakest points in this argument?" or "Play devil's advocate &mdash; why would this fail?" Ask for weaknesses, not opinions.

### Trap Check: Identify the Pattern

Read each scenario and identify which cognitive trap is at play. Select the trap and the appropriate defence.

SCENARIO 1

A marketing lecturer asks AI to explain brand positioning theory. She notices it oversimplifies Keller's framework. Then she asks it to summarise the latest research on supply chain resilience for a cross-disciplinary project. The response sounds thorough and well-referenced, so she copies it directly into her notes.

SCENARIO 2

A researcher shares a conference abstract she drafted using the conversation loop. A colleague reads it, learns AI was involved in the drafting process, and says: "It's well-written but I'm not sure how much of this is actually your thinking." He does not critique any specific argument or claim.

SCENARIO 3

An administrator pitches a new process for handling student complaints to an AI chatbot. The AI responds: "That's a really thoughtful approach! It addresses several key pain points in the current system." She feels validated and proceeds with implementation without seeking critique from colleagues or testing edge cases.
