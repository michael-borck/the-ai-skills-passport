## Research Integrity

Using AI in research comes with disclosure obligations. Journals, funders, and universities increasingly require transparency about how AI was used. Getting this right protects your reputation and your work.

### What you need to know

**Journal policies**
Most major publishers (Elsevier, Springer Nature, Wiley, Taylor & Francis) now require disclosure of AI use in the methods or acknowledgements section. AI cannot be listed as an author. Policies vary by journal, so check before submitting.

**Funder requirements**
ARC and NHMRC have emerging guidance on AI disclosure in grant applications and funded research. Check current guidelines before submitting applications.

**Curtin policy**
Curtin's research integrity framework requires honesty and transparency in all research activities. If AI materially contributed to your work, disclose it. When in doubt, disclose.

**Ethical considerations**
Consider: data privacy (did you upload participant data to AI tools?), reproducibility (can others replicate your AI-assisted process?), and bias (does the AI introduce systematic distortions?).

### Sample disclosure statement

You can adapt this template for your own papers:

"During the preparation of this work, the author(s) used [Tool Name, e.g. ChatGPT-4] for [specific purpose, e.g. improving readability of the manuscript / generating initial code for data analysis / summarising literature during the review phase]. After using this tool, the author(s) reviewed and edited the content as needed and take(s) full responsibility for the content of the publication."

⚠️

**What to watch for**

- Policies are changing rapidly. Check the journal's author guidelines at the time of submission, not months before.

- Using AI for grammar checking (like Grammarly) is generally accepted, but using AI to generate substantial text requires disclosure.

- If you used AI during data analysis, describe exactly what it did and verify the outputs independently.
