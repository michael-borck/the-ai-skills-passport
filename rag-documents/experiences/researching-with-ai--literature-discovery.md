## Literature Discovery

AI tools can help you find relevant papers, summarise abstracts, identify research gaps, and compare methodologies across studies. They work best as a complement to traditional database searches, not a replacement.

### Tools for literature discovery

**Semantic Scholar**
Free academic search engine with AI-powered relevance ranking. Good for finding highly cited papers and tracing citation networks. Shows "TLDR" summaries of abstracts.

**Elicit**
Ask a research question in plain language and Elicit finds relevant papers, extracts key findings, and organises results into a table. Particularly useful for systematic review screening.

**Consensus**
Searches academic papers and synthesises findings into yes/no/mixed answers to your research question. Useful for quickly gauging the state of evidence on a topic.

**ChatGPT / Claude**
General-purpose AI can help brainstorm search terms, suggest related concepts, and summarise papers you paste in. Caution: they can hallucinate citations, so always verify references exist.

### Try this activity

Pick a research topic you are currently working on. Try these steps:

- Search for your topic on **Semantic Scholar** and note the top 5 results

- Ask the same question on **Elicit** and compare which papers appear

- Paste one abstract into **ChatGPT or Claude** and ask: "Summarise the key finding, methodology, and limitations of this study"

- Compare the AI summary against your own reading of the abstract. What did it capture well? What did it miss?

⚠️

**What to watch for**

- AI tools can hallucinate citations (inventing authors, titles, or DOIs that do not exist). Always verify in a real database.

- AI summaries can flatten nuance. A paper's contribution might be its methodology or a specific caveat, not just the headline finding.

- These tools tend to favour English-language, well-cited papers. They may miss important work in other languages or newer publications.
