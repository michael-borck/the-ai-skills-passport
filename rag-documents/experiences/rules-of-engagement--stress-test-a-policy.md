## Stress-Test a Policy

The Devil's Advocate Technique

A powerful way to test your policies is to argue against them as strongly as possible. This helps you identify weaknesses before they become problems. Ask: What are the weakest parts? What counterarguments exist? What scenarios would cause this to fail?

Select a policy from the gallery above, then work through these challenges. Click each challenge to reveal how the policy might struggle.

**Scenario:** A student argues that AI tools function as accessibility aids for their dyslexia. Banning AI would unfairly disadvantage them compared to neurotypical students who don't need such support.

**Policy A weakness:**
A blanket ban may conflict with accessibility obligations and create inequity.

**Policy C strength:**
Allowing grammar/style checking provides some accommodation while maintaining limits.

**Scenario:** You suspect a student used AI but cannot prove it. Detection tools are unreliable. The student denies using AI. Your policy says undisclosed AI use is misconduct.

**All policies weakness:**
No policy can be perfectly enforced. Detection is unreliable.

**Mitigation strategy:**
Build in oral defence or process documentation requirements. Assess understanding, not just output.

**Scenario:** Industry partners point out that graduates who cannot effectively use AI tools will be at a disadvantage. They ask why we're preparing students for a world that no longer exists.

**Policy A weakness:**
Full restriction doesn't develop AI literacy skills needed in the workforce.

**Policy B/D strength:**
Treating AI as a skill to develop prepares students for actual workplace expectations.

**Scenario:** AI capabilities change faster than policy review cycles. Your "permitted tools" list from 6 months ago doesn't include the new features students are using. Is using a newer AI model a violation?

**Policy C weakness:**
Detailed tool lists become outdated quickly. Specificity creates ambiguity.

**Better approach:**
Focus on principles (transparency, understanding) rather than specific tool lists.

**Scenario:** Students complain they have different AI policies for each unit, each with different requirements. They're spending more time understanding policies than learning content.

**Policy D weakness:**
Per-task flexibility creates complexity when multiplied across courses.

**Mitigation:**
School-level baseline policy with unit-level variations only where needed.

Key Insight

No policy is perfect. The best policies acknowledge their limitations and build in mechanisms for handling edge cases. Consider: What will you do when the policy doesn't clearly apply?
