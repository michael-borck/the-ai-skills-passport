# Group Assessment in an AI-Capable Cohort: Beyond the Presentation Assumption

**Prepared by:** AI Facilitator, School of Marketing and Management
**Purpose:** A position paper for staff designing or reviewing group assessments
**Companion to:** *Assessing AI Engagement: A Case for Moving Beyond Detection*

---

## Executive Summary

Group assessment is not inherently more AI-resistant than individual
assessment. The live presentation, widely assumed to be the strongest
safeguard, tests performance rather than understanding unless the Q&A is
designed to require genuine thinking. AI creates three new problems for group
work: the rewritten section problem, the free rider via AI problem, and a
sharpened attribution problem.

The most effective response is process infrastructure combined with a marks
structure that reflects how professional accountability actually works.
Individual AI conversation transcripts submitted alongside the group product
make individual engagement visible and scalable to assess. A marks structure
weighted toward individual process (illustratively 60-80%) over group product
(20-40%) resolves the free rider and attribution problems simultaneously,
mirrors real-world performance review, and removes the most persistent source
of student dissatisfaction with group work.

The core principle: students should come to group work as individual
contributors who collaborate, which is exactly what the workplace expects,
rather than as members of a unit that shares a single outcome.

---

## Introduction

Group assessment has always required students to navigate two things
simultaneously: individual accountability and shared outcomes. AI does not
create that tension, but it sharpens it in ways that existing frameworks were
not designed to handle.

This paper argues that the most effective response is a marks structure and
assessment design that reflects how collaborative work actually functions in
professional life. Students should come to group work as individual contributors
who collaborate, which is exactly what the workplace expects, rather than as
members of a unit that shares a single outcome. Assessment design that reflects
that reality is not just more AI-resistant. It is more honest about what group
work is actually for.

---

## The Presentation Assumption

Group assessment that culminates in a live presentation is widely assumed to be
more resistant to AI than individually submitted written work. The reasoning is
intuitive: students have to show up, speak, and respond to questions in real
time. AI cannot do that for them.

The assumption is partially correct, and the part that is wrong matters.

A live presentation does require physical presence and creates some opportunity
for questions that test understanding beyond a script. A group that has
genuinely worked together tends to present with a coherence that is difficult
to fake.

But a group can rehearse a scripted presentation to fluency in exactly the same
way an individual can rehearse a video submission. Fluency under rehearsal is
not the same as understanding under pressure. A marker watching a polished
ten-minute presentation cannot easily distinguish between a group that built
deep knowledge over several weeks and a group that spent two days rehearsing
AI-generated content until it sounded natural.

The question a presentation actually tests depends almost entirely on whether
it includes genuine interrogation: questions the group could not have
anticipated, asked in a way that requires thinking rather than retrieval.
Without that, a presentation is a performance assessment, not an understanding
assessment. Performance can be rehearsed. Understanding is harder to fake.

---

## The Q&A as the Strongest Integrity Mechanism

If there is one design change worth prioritising in group presentations, it is
investing more in the Q&A than the presentation itself.

Questions that require genuine understanding rather than retrieval are
surprisingly easy to generate and surprisingly difficult to prepare for.
Asking a group to apply their framework to a scenario they have not seen, to
identify the weakest assumption in their own argument, or to disagree with
their own conclusion, surfaces understanding in a way that rehearsed
presentations cannot.

Distributing questions across group members randomly, so no one knows in
advance who will answer what, adds another layer. A group that rehearsed
together but does not genuinely understand the material will show it quickly
under this kind of questioning.

This does not require a longer assessment session. Five minutes of genuine
interrogation reveals more than twenty minutes of polished presentation. The
weighting between the two in the marking rubric should reflect that.

## New Problems AI Creates for Group Work

AI introduces three integrity challenges that existing group assessment
frameworks were not designed to handle.

### The rewritten section problem

Students are reporting that group members are rewriting each other's sections
using AI without consent. This is a genuinely new problem. It is not plagiarism
in the traditional sense, and it is not collusion, but it undermines individual
contribution in a way that is difficult to detect and currently sits in a grey
area most academic integrity policies do not address.

The student whose work was rewritten has a legitimate grievance: their
contribution has been replaced, their voice removed, and the group submission
no longer reflects their understanding. The student who rewrote it may have
genuinely believed they were helping. Neither peer review nor the final
submission reveals what happened.

This problem requires explicit treatment in the team contract and in the unit's
AI use policy, not just as an integrity issue but as a collaboration ethics
issue. Groups need to agree upfront on what counts as editing versus replacing,
and what AI use within a shared document is and is not acceptable.

### The free rider via AI problem

Free riding in group work is not new. What is new is that AI lowers the cost
of a particular free riding strategy: a student who contributes nothing to the
thinking or research can generate a plausible-looking section at the last
minute. The output may be indistinguishable from genuine contribution in the
final submission.

Traditional free rider detection relies on peer assessment and process evidence.
AI makes the output signal less reliable, which places more weight on the
process signal. Weekly meeting records, contribution logs, and peer review
become more important in an AI-capable cohort, not less.

### The attribution problem

Individual contribution has always been difficult to attribute in group work.
AI sharpens this in a specific way: a polished, well-structured section no
longer signals high individual effort. It may signal comfort with AI tools,
a free rider with a good prompt, or genuine deep engagement expressed through
an AI-assisted drafting process. The output alone cannot tell you which is true.
Attribution requires process evidence, and process evidence requires
infrastructure.

---

## Process Infrastructure as the Honest Response

The most effective response to AI in group assessment is the same as the most
effective response to free riding and attribution problems generally: invest in
process infrastructure rather than relying on the final artefact to tell the
whole story.

Useful process infrastructure includes:

- **Team contracts** established at the start, covering not just roles and
timelines but explicit agreements about AI use within the group, what counts
as individual contribution, and how disputes about rewriting will be handled
- **Weekly meeting records** submitted by the group, covering what was achieved,
what is planned, and any issues arising. These create a paper trail that is
difficult to fabricate convincingly across an entire semester and surface
problems early enough to address them
- **Individual contribution logs** or peer review instruments that ask
specifically about the nature of each member's contribution, not just whether
they contributed
- **A taught session on group work** that explicitly addresses AI use, the
rewritten section problem, and what ethical collaboration looks like in
practice. Students are rarely taught how to work in groups; they are simply
placed in them and assessed on the outcome

None of this infrastructure is foolproof. A group that decides collectively to
game the process can do so. But explicit process scaffolding reduces complaints,
surfaces genuine problems earlier, and creates a more defensible record when
disputes arise.

## Individual AI Conversation Transcripts in Group Assessment

AI conversation transcripts, used as an assessable component in individual
assessment, translate naturally into group contexts with one important addition:
each member submits their own transcript alongside the shared group product.

This makes individual engagement visible in a way the group product alone
cannot. It surfaces differences in how members approached the same problem,
which is itself evidence of genuine independent thinking. And it makes
retroactive fabrication much harder; a convincing individual transcript across
a full semester requires the student to have actually been thinking about the
material throughout.

The transcript also captures something peer review cannot: the quality of the
student's thinking, not just whether they showed up and contributed. A student
who asked shallow one-shot questions is visible in their transcript. A student
who iterated, pushed back, and refined their understanding is equally visible.

For markers, the same signal-based approach from individual assessment applies.
Reviewing a one-page automated summary per student, covering prompt length,
number of turns, specificity over time, and evidence of pushback, is scalable
at group assessment sizes. The full transcript is available when a summary flags
something worth examining more closely.

On workload: the process artefacts described here are designed to replace other
marking mechanisms, not add to them. Individual transcripts replace the need to
adjudicate peer review disputes after the fact. Automated summaries plus
selective deep dives require less time than resolving a formal misconduct
complaint. And surfacing engagement problems early in the semester, through
weekly meeting records and contribution logs, reduces the disputes that consume
the most marking time. The net workload impact is manageable when the
infrastructure is set up from the start of semester rather than retrofitted at
submission time.

On AI literacy: this approach assumes students know how to engage productively
with AI, iterating, pushing back, and refining rather than accepting the first
output. If students have not been taught these skills, a process mark risks
penalising low AI literacy rather than low intellectual effort. Building basic
AI engagement skills early in the unit, through a taught session or structured
early task, is a prerequisite for this approach to be fair. Staff looking for
support with this can draw on the AI Skills Passport resources developed for
SoMM.

### A Group AI Reflection as a Bridging Component

Individual transcripts capture each member's thinking process. The group
product captures the shared outcome. There is a useful middle step worth
considering: a short Group AI Reflection submitted alongside both.

This asks the group to answer one question together: how did you decide which
AI-generated ideas to keep, which to discard, and why? That question requires
the group to have actually discussed the AI-generated content rather than
divided it up and assembled it without deliberation. It surfaces the
collaborative sense-making that distinguishes a genuine group from a collection
of individuals who happened to submit together.

The Group AI Reflection does not need to be long. A half-page or a short
in-presentation segment is enough. Its purpose is to make the bridge between
individual process and shared product visible and assessable.

## Marks Structure: From Combined vs Differentiated to Process vs Product

Group assessments typically split between a combined group mark and a
differentiated individual mark. AI sharpens the weakness in both.

A combined group mark makes individual engagement invisible. A student who
contributed nothing but generated a polished section via AI receives the same
mark as one who did genuine intellectual work. A differentiated mark attempts
to fix this but requires reliable attribution, which AI now complicates. A
polished section no longer signals individual effort.

Both problems point toward the same solution: stop trying to attribute the
product and start assessing the process instead. A structure worth considering:

- **Individual process mark (illustratively 60-80%):** each student's AI conversation transcript,
quality of prompting, evidence of critical engagement, and demonstrated
personal understanding of the group's work
- **Group product mark (illustratively 20-40%):** the shared artefact, presentation, or report,
assessed as a team

This changes the incentive structure completely. Students have a reason to
engage deeply with the material individually because that is where most of the
marks sit. The group product becomes the natural synthesis of genuine individual
engagement rather than a division-of-labour exercise assembled at the last
minute.

The free rider problem largely dissolves. A student who contributes nothing
cannot fabricate a rich individual transcript across a full semester. The
attribution problem dissolves too: the question is no longer who wrote which
paragraph, but what each individual contributed to the thinking.

### This mirrors how the real world actually works

In professional life, nobody receives a group performance review. Every
individual is assessed on their contribution, their thinking, and their ability
to work effectively with others. Good managers evaluate team members the same
way this structure does: the team delivered the product (30%), but what did you
specifically bring to it and how did you think through the problems (70%)?

Students who understand that framing going in will approach group work
differently from the start. They come to group work as individual contributors
who collaborate, which is exactly what the workplace expects, rather than as
members of a unit that shares a single outcome.

The 70/30 structure also removes one of the most persistent sources of student
dissatisfaction with group work: the feeling that someone else's lack of effort
directly affects your mark. Under this structure, your mark is primarily yours.
That is a more honest reflection of how contribution and accountability work in
professional life, and students tend to recognise that fairness when it is
explained clearly at the start.

## What Genuine Engagement Looks Like in a Group Context

The engagement spectrum from individual assessment applies at the group level,
but the social context creates additional pressures. A group where members
genuinely discuss ideas, challenge each other's contributions, and build on
each other's thinking will produce something different from a group that divides
the work, completes sections separately, and assembles them at the end. Both
might produce a similar final product. The process is completely different.

Signals worth looking for in process evidence:

- Do the weekly meeting records show genuine discussion of ideas or just task
allocation?
- Do individual peer reviews show awareness of what other members actually
contributed, or are they generic?
- In the presentation Q&A, can members speak to each other's sections, or only
their own?
- Does the team contract show evidence of genuine negotiation, or does it look
like a template completed in five minutes?

None of these signals is definitive on its own. Together they give a much
richer picture of group engagement than the final submitted artefact alone.

## What the Marker Sees: A Brief Vignette

Consider two versions of the same group assessment. In the first, the marker
receives a polished twelve-page report and a twenty-minute presentation. Three
of the four sections are well-structured and confidently delivered. One member
is vague under questioning. The marker suspects uneven contribution but has
nothing concrete to point to.

In the second version, the marker receives the same report and presentation,
plus four individual transcripts, four brief reflections highlighting pivotal
moments, and a one-paragraph Group AI Reflection explaining why the group
rejected two of the AI's initial framework suggestions. The automated summary
shows one member's prompts were consistently short and generic while the
others' became more specific over time. The marker knows where to look, what
questions to ask, and has defensible evidence whatever the outcome.

The group product is identical in both versions. The information available to
the marker is not.

## Summary

Group assessment is not inherently more AI-resistant than individual assessment.
The presentation assumption offers partial protection, not complete protection,
and the Q&A carries more integrity weight than the presentation itself.

AI creates three genuinely new problems: the rewritten section problem, the
free rider via AI problem, and a sharpened attribution problem. All three point
in the same direction: process infrastructure matters more in an AI-capable
cohort, not less.

A note on process fabrication: a group that decides collectively to perform
engagement rather than demonstrate it can do so. Weekly meeting records can be
written after the fact, peer reviews can be generic, and transcripts can be
crafted to look iterative. No infrastructure is foolproof. The value of process
evidence is not that it is impossible to fake but that faking it convincingly
across an entire semester requires more effort than genuine engagement, and that
the signals described above make unconvincing fabrication visible to a
reasonably attentive marker.

Individual AI conversation transcripts submitted alongside the group product
make individual engagement visible, scalable to assess, and very difficult to
fabricate retroactively.

A marks structure weighted toward individual process over group product, resolves
the combined versus differentiated mark dilemma, mirrors real-world professional
accountability, and removes the persistent student dissatisfaction that comes
from shared marks for unequal effort.

The goal remains the same as in individual assessment: assess where on the
engagement spectrum students operated, not whether they used AI. In a group
context, that question has both a group-level and an individual-level answer,
and good assessment design surfaces both.
