The big ideas, even if you do nothing else. Ten principles worth knowing.

Short on time? Overwhelmed by AI? That's fine. If you take nothing else from the AI Skills Passport, take these ten ideas. Each one comes with something you can try in two minutes.

Use AI as a thinking partner, not a vending machine. The value is in the back-and-forth — refining your ideas, challenging assumptions, iterating on drafts. The conversation *is* the work.

Try this (2 min)

Next time you use AI, don't accept the first response. Reply with "What did you assume?" or "What's the weakest part of this?" and see how the output improves.

AI doesn't replace thinking — it amplifies it. Good input produces good output. Lazy input produces polished garbage. The quality of what you bring to the conversation determines the quality of what you get back.

Try this (2 min)

Give AI a vague prompt, then rewrite it with specific context, audience, and purpose. Compare the two outputs. The difference is your thinking.

"Human in the loop" doesn't mean checking AI's work at the end. It means staying in the conversation at every stage. One powerful approach: run two conversations — one where you *think* with AI (explore ideas, challenge assumptions, clarify your question) and one where you *build* with AI (draft, create, produce). You are the bridge. You decide what crosses from thinking to building. Neither conversation has the full picture — only you do. That's what makes you essential, not as a fact-checker, but as the one who holds the context, makes the judgement calls, and connects the dots.

Try this (5 min)

Next time you use AI for something substantial, open two sessions. In the first, explore your question — ask for angles, push back, refine your thinking. Then take what you've landed on and use it to brief the second session for the actual output. Notice how much better the result is when you've done the thinking first.

AI sounds confident even when it's wrong. Before you use what it gives you, **VET** it — like vetting a source:

- **Verify** — Can I find this independently? Check sources, cross-reference.

- **Explain** — Can I explain this in my own words? If not, I don't understand it yet.

- **Test** — Does this hold up under scrutiny? Play devil's advocate, try edge cases.

Try this (10 min)

Ask AI a factual question about your field. Then VET the response: verify a claim independently, explain the answer in your own words, and test it with an edge case.

The best way to get AI output that sounds like you is to start with your own content. Give it your rough notes, your bullet points, your messy first draft. AI is far better at refining your thinking than generating from scratch — and the result sounds like you, not like a machine.

Try this (2 min)

Take something you've already written — an email draft, meeting notes, a paragraph from a report. Paste it into AI and ask "Help me tighten this up." Compare the result to what you'd get from a blank prompt.

Three cognitive traps shape how people relate to AI. **Gell-Mann Amnesia:** you catch AI errors in your area of expertise, then trust it completely on topics you know nothing about. **The AI Dismissal Fallacy:** rejecting an idea solely because AI was involved &mdash; &ldquo;that&rsquo;s just ChatGPT&rdquo; is not a critique. **The Sycophancy Trap:** AI is trained to agree with you, so &ldquo;what do you think?&rdquo; gets you flattery, not feedback.

Try this (2 min)

Next time AI evaluates your work, don&rsquo;t ask &ldquo;what do you think?&rdquo; Instead ask: &ldquo;What are the three weakest points in this?&rdquo; Compare the responses. The difference is the sycophancy you&rsquo;re normally not seeing.

Chaining AI tasks without pushback is the most dangerous way to use AI. The hallucination in step two becomes an assumption in step three, a foundation in step four, and a confident conclusion in step six. Each step builds faithfully on what came before &mdash; and what came before was wrong. The conversation loop prevents this: every iteration is a checkpoint, every pushback is a correction.

Try this (5 min)

Take an AI output you recently accepted. Read it again with one question: &ldquo;What did I assume was correct without checking?&rdquo; Find one claim and verify it independently. That habit is the loop in miniature.

If you want colleagues and collaborators to use AI thoughtfully, show them what that looks like. Be upfront about when and how you used AI. Transparency builds trust and sets the standard — whether you're drafting an email, writing a report, or publishing research.

Try this (2 min)

Add a one-line note to your next document or email: "I used AI to help draft/check/summarise this." Notice how it changes the conversation.

For Teaching

Students need guidance, not prohibition. They're already using AI — the question is whether they're using it well. Meet them where they are and help them develop judgement, not just compliance.

Try this (2 min)

Ask a student how they're using AI right now. Listen without judgement. You'll learn more in two minutes than from any report.

It's different from how you learned, but the thinking still happens. When a student uses AI to explore, question, and refine their understanding, the conversation is the evidence of learning. The process matters more than the product.

Try this (2 min)

Pick a topic you know well. Ask AI to explain it, then critique the response. Notice how evaluating AI output requires genuine understanding.
